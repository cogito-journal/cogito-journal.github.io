An Epistemological Investigation of Geometry


Adriraj Talukdar



The Straight Line

What is a straight line in Euclid's Elements? Euclid described straight line as breadthless length which lies equally with respect to all the points on itself. It is said to be straightest (nowhere curved) path between two points. Its uniqueness is assumed in the postulate : Given two points there exists a unique straight line joining them. Now it is easy to relate this description to our perception of straight line. But in a formal sense, this does not qualify as a definition. Here the straight line, or rather the notion of straightness is a primitive one.

On the other hand, there is another approach forwarded by Descartes, Hilbert, Riemann and others - the metric approach. In Euclid's system it is proved by the fundamental postulates and various results such as triangle inequality that the straight line is the shortest path between two points in 2 or 3 dimensions. However, in metric approach, primitively straight line is defined to be the path between two points which has shortest distance. Now, this distance is denoted by a metric function which gives non-negative real values and follows triangle inequality. And in ℝ2 or ℝ3 the chosen metric is the Cartesian distance and it works fine with our general intuition of physical space. All the results of Euclid's Elements in ℝ2 or ℝ3 hold perfectly by starting from metric too. So one might argue that Euclid's geometric space is an immediate result of us being submerges in the physical space which has all the same properties. Obviously in Newtonian mechanics space is isometric, homogenous and Euclidean and that never worked against intuition. But today we know of numerous geometries and discuss about larger classes of geometries among which Euclid is just a special case. We can talk about geometries where parallel lines are not always equidistant or sum of internal angles of a triangle is not twice a right angle. One might argue that we have developed some analytic tools and now we can generalise the results of Euclid to larger classes of geometries and can think of different approaches to form such geometries. But however, Euclid's geometry is the natural one because it does not require any training in abstract logic or mathematics, even the uninitiated child intuitively think that physical space is Euclidean.

Now let us look at the Relativity Theory of Einstein. Einstein considers space-time to be nonEuclidean. Experiments have proven that in presence of heavy object light rays can bend. In general relativity gravitational force is caused by curvatures in space-time which cause objects to move in certain directions (along the curved path). By the principle of least action, the geodesics or shortest path in its initial direction is followed by a travelling ray of light. The fact that it bends near heavy objects indicate that the geodesics are not straight lines there, rather they are themselves curved. But Einstein showed that space-time follows a hyperbolic geometry. However, space without the time coordinate (or better to say space at a given time instance) is still Euclidean. But universe is expanding and if we incorporate time only then the four dimensional space-time continuum is nonEuclidean.


The Problem of Primitive Notions

Now if we look at the approach in Elements, we agree to the description of straight line because we can associate with it our understanding of straight line from experience. However there is no definite affirmation in saying the straightest path between to points. Straightness implies there is no change in direction. It is true that change of direction can be understood by angles in 2D. But again angle is determined by two straight lines and direction lies just along a straight line. One can see the circularity of establishing primitive notions in pure analytic terms. The same is true when we understand straight line by shortest distance. We can measure distance only by using preferably a rigid body as a unit - distance is understood as nothing but length vis-a-vis the idea of line segment. In this sense, geometry initiates from synthetic knowledge. But we know that we can not draw a straight line or circle or any of the nice figures in geometry up to infinite precision in real life. Then what do we really understand by straight line or circle if we can not exactly identify the object of geometry (say a straight line proper) and the object of perception (say the straight line drawn on paper with very small yet positive breadth)? Plato had an answer for this. Plato said that the idea of straightness or circleness (the property of being a circle) reside in our mind as pure idea and all the physical lines or circles are just realization of these ideas. Circles or straight lines are not in the physical space, there are only distortions or manifestations of the pure ideal. Plato's essential idea was that the Real is subject to knowledge (say, Euclidean Geometry) and it is only our empirical investigation (say, Visual Geometry) that leads to distortion from the Real.


The Kantian Argument

German philosopher Immanuel Kant demonstrated a flaw in Plato’s scheme. Abstract reasoning must be independent of individual human understanding. Therefore we can not have a priori abstract knowledge about the Real which we can forward to further reasoning. It is only through passive observation of sensible world and active synthesis of the sensible data can knowledge initiate and only then we can understand the a priori form of our understanding by reflecting upon the process of the aforementioned synthesis. Kant said that the conception of space, however, is synthetic a priori. By means of externality we have the intuition about space – which is realised as the difference in place between objects external to us as opposed to their difference in their properties within themselves. Kant establishes the limit of human understanding to be the horizon of the possibilities of sensibility. So straight line or any geometric object, Kant says, is the idealisation of some observable objects synthesized through our intuition of space. So the Kantian take would be as following: Our intuition of space that is a priori within the form of understanding makes us see the physical reality in a Euclidean three dimensional space. Euclid reflects on the most fundamental intuition about geometric objects which he can do by associating our experience of the physical correspondents of those objects with their abstract form in pure geometry. This intuitive association is established by the aforementioned primitive notions. However, no claims can be made about the Truth of the pure physical space.


Real Is Rational And Rational Is Real

Plato and Kant both commits to one thing - that is the Real exists independent of us - static, perfect and complete; and we by our sensory and analytic apparatus try to understand it. Kant further says, it is impossible to know the Real as it is. Another German philosopher Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel put a different question before us - what exactly is this Real? That the thing-in-itself beyond our sensible possibility is unknowable is a knowledge of the thing-in-itself itself. By this argument Hegel rejects the possibility of absolute unknowability. The Real is not the absolute state of things in themselves, rather it is the dynamicity of the things. Our experience encounters premises that are at the same time opposite but each exists only with respect to the other - they are each others' negation and affirmation. It is in our understanding that we synthesize them in order to resolve the contradiction and then move onto the next agenda. This is the scheme of the Real vis-a-vis the Rational - the scheme of Dialectics. So let us now try to apply this to questions regarding geometry and space.

Take the straight line. Have you ever drawn a perfect straight line? Or have anyone ever perceived perfect breadthless length? It is not possible (The finest microscope can not see breadthlessness). Then how do we understand what Euclid meant by straight line without any hardship? One thing that we obviously experience physically is the sense of direction (simply by the ability to point out at something). So now if there are two objects and I start from one pointing towards the other I can walk along a path to reach the other object (Let us forget for this moment that I am walking on a non-planar earth) while pointing to it constantly. It is a very concrete experience. Now since we can talk about constant direction, we have to consider the possibility of changing direction as well. This requires at least a (planar) surface where infinitely many directions are possible. These directions are distinct insofar that they point away from each other, but they are also similar since property of space itself is unaltered along any one of them. So the notion of the straightness is the negation of possibilities of direction and at the same time affirmation of a particular direction.

So now we have the straight in straight line. But before that we have to figure out what is line. Euclid defined line as a breadthless length. So let us understand the notion of distance or length. And distance can be understood in terms of duration. Kant said that time is the a priori intuition of our internality -realised as the order or interval in which we perceive several events either among external objects or internal thought. Now if we start from one point and reach another, it will involve a time duration to travel the path. Now if we measure the distance along this path with a rigid body such as a ruler, both end points of that ruler is determined simultaneously. In this sense distance becomes both preservation of the information about the duration as well as its negation. And up to this the notion of length is clear to us. Now what do we understand by saying the length is breadthless? We said straightness is determination of one direction along all the points on the path. But for any line, straight or curved, breadthlessness indicated the determination of unique direction at a given point. That is, the direction allowed on point can be different from another point, but that one point must allow only one direction. And then we have breadthlessness. So once through determination of direction and then through determination of duration we reach at an object which is a breadthless length that lies equally to all points on it that is the Straight Line. When we say straight line extends infinitely in both direction we simply mean that if our space allows we can keep walking on a fixed direction forever, without stopping. It is also not hard to say that shortest path between two points is the path where it takes least time to travel that is along the path where we do not change the direction multiple times. So the metric intuition is equally sound in our scheme of determination of direction and duration.

Interestingly if we are given two points quite far away, it is unlikely we can draw the (physical) straight line joining them in the first attempt. It is easier to find first the shortest path and then claim it is the straightest path. However intuitively we know about the straightest path first and then conclude it is shortest. Thus Euclid's approach was more intuitive and experiential. But Descartes's approach is much more sophisticated and precise since it builds itself on Euclidean synthetic intuition combined with analytic method of Algebra. Let us reserve the dialogue between Algebra and Geometry for another essay.


Addendum

The Hegelian scheme equates reality not with the hidden truth behind appearance. There is no true reality deep beneath the surface. It's all on the surface. And all of it is in motion, the universe is becoming, insofar that it is Not absolute, static, permanent, perfect. Universe itself is imperfect similar to our cognition of it. This is quite visible in our development from Newtonian mechanics to theory of relativity. Newton considered the physical space to be independent of us - homogenous, static, absolute. And time is also absolute insofar that a given time interval is same for every object in the universe. Einstein showed that space and time is relativistic. But not only that. Let us imagine there is a point mass. Mass corresponds to inertia - that resists change in motion. Acceleration is change in motion and thus a negation to mass. So for this two concrete category to be synthesized Newton defined force as the external agent causing the change. Newton went farther to demonstrate that any object with mass exerts a gravitational force on other masses. However he could not elucidate what causes this gravitational force. Einstein surprisingly showed that it is space-time itself, or rather curvature in space-time caused by mass that causes gravitational force. But then what is mass - that what causes curvature in space-time. To simplify all these is as such: Newton had absolute Euclidean space and absolute time and also mass and gravitational force caused by mass. Einstein then sublated absolute space and time in non-Euclidean relativistic space-time and sublated mass and gravity in curvature of the same space-time. And thus we are here with a better developed, sophisticated model of the universe, with a better understanding of reality.
